The Whole World in One Morning
The moment Murphy's paws hit the sand, he forgot everything — the car ride, the leash clicking onto his collar, the word stay that humans seemed so fond of. There was only this: the cold, wet shock of the shoreline rushing over his feet and the enormous, incomprehensible smell of the ocean.
He ran. There was no other option.
He ran until the water swallowed his legs and the foam surprised him with its softness. He ran back toward the dry sand when a wave chased him — then turned and charged it right back, barking once as if to establish terms. The wave ignored him. He took this as a victory.
His owner, Elena, sat on a piece of driftwood with her coffee going cold, watching him investigate a clump of seaweed with the seriousness of a detective. He sniffed it from four angles, pawed it once, and then looked up at her with an expression that said you need to see this.
She came over. She looked at the seaweed. "Wow," she said.
Murphy wagged his tail so hard his whole back half swayed. That was all he needed — for someone else to know about the seaweed.
By the time they headed back to the car, Murphy was soaked, sandy to his shoulders, and carrying a piece of driftwood three times wider than his head. He had no plan for it. He simply could not leave it behind.
Elena opened the trunk and looked at him. He looked at her, wood clenched proudly in his teeth.
She moved her jacket. "Fine," she said. "It comes with us."
Murphy climbed in, turned two circles, and collapsed with a groan of pure satisfaction. The beach receded behind the rear window. He was asleep before they reached the main road, dreaming, probably, of the wave he had almost definitely beaten.
